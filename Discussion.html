Discussion

Discussion

We found that there was no significant difference in the body image scores across the groups, this means that we are unable to conclude that the photos from the different Instagram hashtags had an effect on the body image of our participants. This differs from previous studies which have found that viewing images of thin women negatively impacts a woman's body satisfaction (Groesz et al., 2001; Moreno-Domínguez et al., 2019; Rounds and Stutts, 2021), while viewing images of plus-sized or average-sized women positively impacted a woman's body satisfaction (Moreno-Domínguez et al., 2019' Groesz et al., 2001). It also goes against Rounds and Stutts's (2021) finding that viewing photos to the #fitspiration page on Instagram negatively impacted body satisfaction of the women participating, which were photos that also images of thin or toned women in workout clothing just as the images that we selected from our study were.

Our findings do support Groesz et al.'s (2001) finding that the body satisfaction of girls under the age of 19 was more impacted by viewing images of thin women than that of women who are 19-years-old or older. More research should be done to determine why age seems to play a role. Future studies could look into the role of media literacy, and how our understandings of the ability of social media users to edit their images before posting may increase as we get older. Additionally, future research could investigate if our overexposure to social media and the beauty standard—which is suspected by researchers to be the cause of the negative body satisfaction reported after viewing images of thin women (Moreno-Domínguez et al., 2019; Niu et al., 2020; Groesz et al., 2001; Rounds and Stutts, 2021; Eisend and Möller, 2007)—may reduce the impact that it has.

It is also possible that recruiting students from a Christian university impacted our results. A past study found that having strong religious beliefs and a solid relationship with God was correlated with less body image concerns, while a "superficial faith coupled with a doubtful and anxious relationship with God" was correlated with higher levels of body image concern (Akrawi et al., 2015, p. 7). 90.9% of our participants identified as Catholic or Christian, and around 65% rated themselves as ranging from moderately to very religious. Future research could compare the scores across a variety of college campuses, to compare if being in a religious setting impacts body image.

Finally, it is also noted that our travel group had the highest PASTAS scores of the three conditions. While this difference was insignificant, it is possible that these photos may have elicited thoughts about travel that increased body concerns. Future research could look into whether viewing landscape images leads to thoughts of how self-conscious a person feels on an airplane, walking around unfamiliar areas, or how they feel in clothing that may be the expected attire for the location (e.g., a bikini at the beach).

Limitations and Future Directions

         Our experiment had some limitations. Our sample size was relatively small, which resulted in a high standard deviation between the Physical Appearance State and Trait Anxiety Scale scores. A higher sample size would have been preferable, and may have reduced the standard deviation or changed the means of the scores.

         As noted above, future research could investigate if the impact on body image caused by social media is reduced with age because we become desensitized, the effect of media literacy on the impact of social media to our body image, the role of religion, and the impact of landscape images. Studies could further investigate the role of age by comparing the results of different age groups to each other to see how the impact of social media on body image may change as we age. In addition, it may also be beneficial to have participants complete the PASTAS test before and after viewing the photos in order to see what may have been a direct influence of the photos they were exposed to.

Conclusion

This study tested the effect of images from three different Instagram hashtags on women's perception of their body. Our results indicated that there was no significant influence of the posts on the women's feelings about their body. We suspect that this finding may be because of our mean age, small sample size, or the religious nature of the university. Further research should be conducted to determine the influence of these factors.
